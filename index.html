Introduction to Professional Skills

Introduction

The transition from academic life to the professional corporate environment requires a massive shift in mindset, behavior, and expectations. This introductory lecture provided a foundational understanding of what it truly takes to succeed beyond formal education. The most critical insight was that while technical abilities are essential for securing employment, long-term career success and promotions depend almost entirely on the continuous development of soft skills.

Reflective Analysis & Content

This session encouraged me to reflect deeply on my inner psychological framework. I learned that my professional output is governed by:

Values — My moral compassBeliefs — My convictionsAttitudes — How I react to difficultiesCharacter — Consistent ethical qualities

The most eye-opening activity was the Johari Window. By analyzing myself, I discovered my "Blind Spots" — qualities and habits that my peers can clearly see, but I am entirely oblivious to. Instead of viewing these blind spots as terrifying weaknesses, I now view them as prime opportunities for growth. Seeking feedback is the only way to move traits from the Blind Spot into the Open Area.

Application of Soft Skills

I have already begun applying self-awareness from the Johari Window exercise to my university group work. Instead of hiding my struggles with a particular coding module, I am now open about my workload, which improves team communication and trust.

I now categorize my professional development into three distinct buckets:

01Technical Skills — Java, React, Excel
02Soft Skills — Communication, empathy, listening
03Transferable Skills — Adaptability, critical thinking

By structuring my learning this way, I can actively track which areas of my professional persona need the most urgent improvement before I graduate.

Conclusion

Before this lesson, I falsely believed that professional success depended solely on being the best programmer in the room. I now understand that without communication, teamwork, and accountability, it is impossible to thrive in a collaborative tech environment. Continuously developing these skills will make me a confident, adaptable, and highly sought-after IT graduate.

Emotional Intelligence

RJ
Mrs. Rubika Joshi
Lecturer

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

In the beginning of this session, the core concept established was the critical difference between IQ (Intelligence Quotient) and EQ (Emotional Intelligence). While IQ might help us secure a job by proving our technical competence, it is EQ that helps us keep the job, navigate office politics, and grow into leadership roles. Emotion is not just a fleeting feeling; it encompasses our thoughts, physiological body reactions, and outward behaviors. For example, when an IT system crashes, the anxiety we feel physically alters how we respond to our teammates.

Reflective Analysis & Content

This session made me deeply reflect on my emotional behavior. I realized that possessing strong programming skills is entirely useless if I cannot manage my emotions during stressful deployment deadlines.

We heavily discussed Daniel Goleman's five domains of emotional intelligence:

Self-awarenessSelf-regulationMotivationEmpathySocial Skills
“Anyone can become angry — that is easy. But to be angry with the right person, and to the right degree, and at the right time, and for the right purpose, and in the right way — that is not easy.”— Aristotle

Application of Soft Skills

I can directly apply these concepts to my academic group projects and future IT career. For example, in a recent project, instead of aggressively presenting technical errors to a team member, I utilized empathy and social skills to understand their perspective and communicated the necessary fixes constructively.

Furthermore, I am now actively practicing cognitive reappraisal and mindfulness when debugging code. Rather than reacting with immediate frustration to a compiler error, I take a step back, regulate my emotional response, and approach the problem with a calm, analytical mindset. This severely reduces team conflict and improves collaborative efficiency.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this session proved that success in the IT industry relies just as heavily on emotional control as it does on logic and algorithms. Learning to manage stress, accept constructive feedback without defensiveness, and maintain a positive outlook will make me a significantly more effective professional.

The Five DomainsEmotional LeakageIQ vs EQSelf-AwarenessConflict Resolution

Introduction

At the beginning of this lecture, my knowledge of portfolios was highly limited; I assumed a portfolio was merely a physical folder where artists stored their drawings. My perception was completely transformed. A professional portfolio is a purposeful, curated selection of an individual's work, explicitly designed to demonstrate their skills, achievements, and evolutionary progress over time. It is a critical tool for IT students to prove their practical capabilities to employers.

Reflective Analysis & Content

Having gained this knowledge, I began analyzing my own academic habits. It became painfully clear that although I regularly complete complex coding assignments and database projects, I never store them properly. Once graded, they are forgotten. Because of this, I have no tangible way to trace my technical development or prove my skills to an internship recruiter.

I learned that a portfolio is not just a data dump of old documents; it requires deep reflection. A true portfolio consists of the actual work paired with reflective statements explaining what the project is, why it was included, the challenges faced, and the specific technologies utilized.

Application of Soft Skills

As an IT student, I will immediately begin curating an Employment Portfolio. I plan to include:

Website Assignments (HTML/CSS/JS)Database Architecture DiagramsCybersecurity Analysis ReportsLive GitHub Repositories

The lecture highlighted several vital stages: defining the purpose, identifying the audience, choosing the format, organizing the content, and maintaining a highly professional appearance. I will use platforms like GitHub Pages or WordPress to host my portfolio.

Conclusion

In summary, a portfolio is the ultimate evidence of employability. It bridges the gap between claiming you know how to code on a CV and actually proving it. Moving forward, I will actively maintain my digital portfolio, regularly updating it with new projects and reflections, ensuring I am always prepared to showcase my true value to the corporate world.

Crafting an Effective CV

IR
Dr. (Ms). Ishara Ravihare
Lecturer in English, Academic, and Researcher | SLIIT City University / Curtin Colombo

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

Unlike highly theoretical modules, this session provided immediate, practical knowledge on how to construct a Curriculum Vitae (CV) and a tailored Cover Letter. A CV is a comprehensive marketing document detailing your educational background, professional qualifications, and specific technical skills. Because modern recruiters spend an average of only 6 to 10 seconds scanning a CV before making a decision, understanding how to structure this document is a vital survival skill in the job market.

Reflective Analysis & Content

Prior to this session, I had drafted a basic CV, but after learning the industry standards, I realized it was deeply flawed. I failed to include a Professional Summary at the top. My bullet points were entirely too generic — I simply listed "wrote code" without measurable achievements.

I also learned the absolute necessity of customizing a CV for every single job application to pass through automated Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS).

When writing a Cover Letter, we were taught the standard 5-paragraph structure:

1State the specific position applied for
2Mention recent educational qualifications
3Highlight relevant project experiences
4Emphasize transferable soft skills
5Close with a confident call to action

Application of Soft Skills

I have since completely overhauled my CV. I improved the visual appearance by utilizing clear, bold headings, organized bullet points, and proper white spacing. I replaced generic responsibilities with action-oriented achievements (e.g., "Developed an MVC-based Java application that improved workflow efficiency").

I also applied strict formatting consistency, ensuring the font style and size remain identical throughout. I now approach Cover Letters not as a repetitive chore, but as a strategic opportunity to tell the story of why I am passionate about a specific company's mission.

Conclusion

This session was invaluable in teaching me how to market myself effectively. I learned the correct structural rules, identified and corrected my formatting mistakes, and understood the psychology of the recruiter. By mastering the art of the CV and Cover Letter, I have significantly increased my chances of securing high-quality interviews in the IT sector.

ATS OptimizationProfessional SummariesTailoring ContentCover Letter RulesClear Formatting

Introduction

This relies heavily on professional judgment, courageous communication, and strict accountability.

We also reviewed the IEEE and ACM Codes of Ethics, which demand that we prioritize public safety above all else, reject bribery, avoid conflicts of interest, and continuously improve our technical competence.

Conclusion

This session profoundly enhanced my understanding of professionalism in computing. I learned that my future employability depends not just on my ability to write efficient algorithms, but on my integrity and ethical awareness. Protecting users, maintaining absolute honesty, and making the difficult but correct ethical decisions under pressure are the true marks of a trustworthy IT professional.

Interview Skills

Dr. (Ms). Ishara Ravihare
Lecturer in English, Academic, and Researcher | SLIIT City University / Curtin Colombo

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

Following the CV writing session, this lecture focused on the critical next stage of employability: the job interview. Job interviews are a mutual process of evaluation. While the employer evaluates the applicant's skills, knowledge, and personality traits, the applicant must simultaneously evaluate the company's culture and terms to ensure it is a proper fit. Success relies heavily on mastering both verbal and non-verbal communication.

Reflective Analysis & Content

The most startling revelation was that a staggering 93% of our message is delivered non-verbally (through body language, posture, and tone of voice), while only 7% is communicated through actual spoken words.

We also learned the STAR Method for answering complex behavioral questions:

SSituation — Outline the context
TTask — Define the goal
AAction — Detail your steps
RResult — Quantifiable outcome

Application of Soft Skills

I will directly apply these techniques to my upcoming internship interviews. By using the STAR approach, I can completely eliminate the anxiety of "rambling." Instead of preparing canned, robotic responses, I can dynamically structure real-world experiences into compelling, concise stories that prove my capabilities.

Furthermore, I am actively practicing the 6 C's of Effective Communication:

ClearConciseCorrectCompleteConcreteCourteous

Conclusion

Success in interviews is not about perfectly reciting technical definitions; it is about projecting confidence, active listening, and structured storytelling. Identifying my previous weaknesses, such as poor eye contact when nervous, has allowed me to practice and improve. The STAR method is an invaluable tool that will support my professional journey for years to come.

Meetings & Minutes Writing

IR
Dr. (Ms). Ishara Ravihare
Lecturer in English, Academic, and Researcher | SLIIT City University / Curtin Colombo

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

Meetings are the central nervous system of professional corporate communication. They are essential for sharing ideas, resolving complex blockers, making strategic decisions, and building team cohesion. However, in the IT industry, meetings are incredibly expensive in terms of labor hours. This lecture emphasized that meetings are not casual conversations; they require intense preparation, strict discipline, active listening, and concise documentation.

Reflective Analysis & Content

Prior to this lecture, I viewed meetings simply as informal discussions where talking loud and often was all that mattered. I have come to realize that dominating a conversation without actively listening severely damages the quality of the discussion.

We analyzed the distinct differences between good and bad meeting behavior. Good participants strictly obey ground rules, keep to the topic, use clear language, and allow others to finish speaking without interruption. Crucially, a successful meeting requires an Agenda distributed beforehand.

We also covered the art of Minute Writing. Minutes are not a verbatim transcript; they are a formalized, legal summary of the meeting, capturing exactly what was decided and the specific Action Items assigned to individuals with rigid deadlines.

Application of Soft Skills

I can immediately apply these skills to my university group software projects. During sprint planning meetings, I will ensure we stay focused by utilizing a strict agenda. If a teammate suggests an approach I disagree with, I will use polite, professional phrasing: "I understand your point, but perhaps we can try another approach because it may save processing time," rather than "Your idea is wrong."

Furthermore, I will volunteer to take the meeting minutes. By officially documenting who is responsible for which coding task and by what date, I can dramatically improve our team's accountability and eliminate confusion.

Conclusion

This lesson was highly practical for my development as an IT student. I will spend my career attending client discussions, daily stand-ups, and bug-fixing meetings. Understanding how to prepare for a meeting, conduct myself respectfully, and document outcomes via minutes ensures I will project competence and reliability to my future employers.

Agenda PreparationActive ListeningAction Items & DeadlinesProfessional DisagreementTime Management

7 Steps to Perfect Telephone Etiquette

IR
Dr. (Ms). Ishara Ravihare
Lecturer in English, Academic, and Researcher | SLIIT City University / Curtin Colombo

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

Despite the dominance of email and instant messaging, the telephone remains an incredibly powerful tool for immediate, complex problem-solving in the IT sector. Because voice calls strip away all visual body language, your tone, clarity, and professionalism must carry 100% of your message. A single telephone call can form an immediate, lasting first impression of both you and your entire organization.

Reflective Analysis & Content

I realized during this session that, due to the prevalence of texting, I had developed extremely poor telephone habits. I often answered calls with a blunt, suspicious "Hello?" and rushed through conversations. The lecture explicitly detailed that a poor, impatient tone on the phone is universally interpreted as disrespect.

We learned the 7 Steps to Perfect Telephone Etiquette:

1Answering promptly (within 3 rings)
2Identifying yourself and your department immediately
3Speaking clearly and smiling
4Actively listening without interrupting
5Asking for permission before placing someone on hold
6Taking meticulous notes during the call
7Closing professionally by summarizing next steps

Application of Soft Skills

These skills are directly applicable to IT Helpdesk roles, client consulting, and remote team collaborations. Moving forward, I will completely banish casual greetings in professional contexts. I will answer calls with a calm, structured greeting: "Good morning, IT Support, this is Upendra. How may I help you?"

When troubleshooting a technical issue over the phone, I will ensure I speak slowly, avoid confusing IT jargon, and regularly use active listening markers (like "I understand") to reassure the client that I am fully engaged in solving their problem.

Conclusion

Mastering telephone etiquette is about projecting competence and empathy using nothing but sound. By adhering to the 7-step framework, I have eliminated my anxiety regarding professional calls. I now view the telephone not as an interruption, but as a strategic tool to build immediate rapport, de-escalate frustrated clients, and resolve complex technical issues swiftly.

Prompt AnsweringSmiling While SpeakingActive Voice ListeningProfessional Sign-offsClear Voicemails

The Art of Negotiation

MA
Mrs. S. Maneesha Aishcharya
MBA (PIM-USJ), B.Sc. Business Administration (Special) (First Class) (USJ)

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

Through this highly engaging session, we were introduced to the structured art of negotiation. Negotiation is not a hostile battle; it is a systematic process where two or more parties work collaboratively to reach a mutually acceptable decision. It relies heavily on advanced communication skills, rapid problem-solving, and strict emotional control. It is an indispensable employability skill for future team workers, interviewees, and managers.

Reflective Analysis & Content

During the lecture, I was deeply fascinated by the core principle: "People before Problems." This principle made me realize that successful negotiation requires completely separating human emotions from the logistical problem itself. Instead of concentrating on personal conflicts, professionals focus strictly on finding a logical solution that satisfies underlying interests.

The most powerful concept introduced was BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement). BATNA represents your ultimate fallback plan if negotiations fail entirely. Understanding that a thoroughly researched, strong BATNA provides massive confidence and bargaining power fundamentally changed how I view high-stakes discussions.

Application of Soft Skills

Negotiation is a highly transferable soft skill that I will use daily in my IT career. During group assignments, team members frequently clash over deadlines, technical architectures, and task distribution. By applying active listening, empathy, and "win-win" thinking, I can effectively manage these disagreements without destroying team morale.

Rather than focusing solely on my personal benefit, I will aim to create solutions that satisfy both parties. If I am negotiating a salary or a software contract in the future, I will approach the table with objective criteria, seeking to build long-term professional relationships.

PreparationActive ListeningWin-Win MindsetEmotional Control

Conclusion

This session proved that negotiation is an essential life skill associated with confidence and strategic decision-making. I learned that true success requires deep preparation, the ability to focus on mutual interests rather than rigid positions, and the emotional intelligence to maintain respect under pressure.

People Before ProblemsBATNA StrategyWin-Win MindsetExtensive PreparationEmotional Control

Introduction

In this highly practical session, we explored the critical importance of personal grooming and etiquette in social and corporate environments. ?What is the occasion?
?Who will be present?
?What is the expected culture?

Conclusion

This session was a paradigm shift. Professionalism is not measured by the cost of your suit, but by your cleanliness, your meticulous preparation, and your respect for the people around you. By mastering first impressions, boundary setting, and digital etiquette, I am actively building a powerful, highly respectable personal brand that will serve me throughout my entire career.

Dining Etiquette & Table Manners

SN
Mrs. Samudini Nagasena
Educator | Aspiring Scrum Evangelist | MSc Project Management

Scroll to explore ↓

Introduction

While it may seem unusual for an IT curriculum, dining etiquette is an exceptionally important soft skill. Corporate life involves networking lunches, interview dinners, banquets, and client meetings. This session emphasized that proper table manners are not about rigid snobbery; they are about projecting confidence, showing deep respect for your host, and demonstrating intense social discipline during high-stakes business occasions.

Reflective Analysis & Content

First impressions are formed blindingly fast, and the way an individual behaves at a dining table reveals volumes about their personality, refinement, and respect for others.

The most practical takeaway was the BMW Rule for navigating a formal table layout:

BBread · Always on your Left
MMeal · In the Middle
WWater · Always on your Right

We also learned the crucial "Outside-In" rule for utensils — start with the cutlery furthest from your plate and work inward with each course.

Application of Soft Skills

I now feel equipped to handle formal dining situations with absolute confidence. When attending a business lunch, I will arrive 5-10 minutes early, strictly follow the dress code, and wait for the host's instructions before seating myself.

•Use the "Outside-In" rule for utensils
•No elbows on the table
•No smartphones on the table — ever
•Use "Please" and "Thank you" generously
•Wait for the host before starting

Conclusion

This session proved that dining etiquette is a vital employability skill. Good table manners are a universal sign of respect and cultural awareness, applicable in both formal corporate events and everyday social situations. Because I am no longer distracted by the anxiety of making a table-setting mistake, I can focus 100% of my energy on networking, holding great conversations, and advancing my career.
